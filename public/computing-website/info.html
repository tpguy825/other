Like normal border collies, short-haired ones have black-and-white or brown coats, with distinctive markings, which can be recognised from far away. There are also bi-coloured border collies (2 colours) and tri-coloured ones (3 colours), such as the the example here.

Collies are famous for ‘the eye’: a nearly unblinking stare. As scary as it is to humans, it is designed for use on livestock, who tend to make eye contact with the dog as it moves in to herd them along. A sheep or cow fixed with the eye will feel threatened and shy away, helping drive the rest of the herd in the desired direction.

Collies are a highly energetic breed, with all the strength and motivation needed to move herds of livestock across broad stretches of grazing land. Because of this, they are not often recommended as house pets. Left unsupervised, collies will usually become quite destructive to release their energy, with a high chance of things being shredded (such as pillows and toys)

This extra energy is helped to be calmed by the fact that collies are incredibly intelligent and very receptive to training. They are a working breed, after all, and are eager to work hard. Collies can be taught to herd livestock or chase down pests easily, and lots of intensive training can make them suitable for home protection, medic alert, or helping disabled people.
